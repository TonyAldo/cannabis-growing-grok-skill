# Photoperiod Cannabis Growing Guide

## What makes photoperiods different

Photoperiod cannabis plants respond to the light/dark cycle to determine when to flower. As long as they receive more than ~14 hours of light per day, they remain in vegetative growth. When the light cycle shifts to 12 hours on / 12 hours off, flowering is triggered. This gives the grower complete control over plant size, shape, and timing.

**Key characteristics:**
- Veg indefinitely under 18+ hours of light — grow as big or small as you want
- Flower when flipped to 12/12 (indoors) or as days shorten in fall (outdoors)
- Can be cloned — take cuttings from a vegging plant to produce genetically identical copies
- Can maintain mother plants for ongoing clone production
- Can be revegged after harvest (with patience)
- More forgiving of training stress — recovery time is unlimited in veg
- Generally higher yield potential per plant than autoflowers
- Longer total grow time (3-6+ months depending on veg duration)

## Light schedules

**Vegetative phase:**
| Schedule | Use Case |
|---|---|
| 18/6 | Standard — good balance of growth and energy cost |
| 20/4 | Faster growth, common in commercial settings |
| 24/0 | Rarely used for photos; debatable benefits, high cost |

**Flowering phase:**
- **12/12** — Standard. Non-negotiable for most strains. Some equatorial sativas may need 11/13 or 10/14 to finish.
- Light leaks during the dark period can cause re-vegging or hermaphroditism. The dark period must be truly dark — even a small LED indicator or light bleed from a poorly sealed tent can be enough to cause problems.

**When to flip:**
There's no universal answer — it depends on your space, desired plant size, and training strategy. Key considerations:

- Plants typically stretch 50-100% during the first 2-3 weeks of flower (the "stretch"). A plant that's 18 inches at flip may reach 30-36 inches by the time stretch ends.
- If growing in a tent, flip when the plant is at 40-50% of your maximum desired height.
- Sea of green (SOG): short or no veg, flip early, many small plants
- ScrOG: longer veg to fill the net, flip once the screen is ~70% full
- If running multiple strains with different stretch characteristics, plan around the tallest/stretchiest one.

## Cloning

Cloning is one of the biggest advantages of photoperiod growing. A successful clone is genetically identical to the mother plant, giving you consistent results run after run — same potency, terpene profile, growth structure, yield potential, and flowering time.

### Why cloning works

Cannabis cells near a wound site can differentiate into root tissue, driven by auxins (plant hormones that regulate cell elongation and root initiation). Rooting hormones supplement this natural process to speed things up. Clones grow faster than seedlings in early development because they originate from mature tissue rather than embryonic growth — they're already "adult" plants from a cellular perspective.

### Taking clones — the cutting technique

1. Select a healthy branch tip from a vegging mother — 4-6 inches with 2-3 nodes
2. Cut at a 45° angle just below a node with a **sterile** razor blade or scalpel (not scissors — they crush the stem and damage vascular tissue)
3. Immediately place the cut end in water or cloning solution — don't let air enter the stem
4. Remove lower leaves, leaving 2-3 sets of fan leaves at the top. Trim large fan leaves in half to reduce transpiration (the clone has no roots to pull water, so it relies on leaf absorption)
5. Dip the cut end in rooting hormone (gel preferred over powder — better contact and coverage)
6. Insert into your chosen rooting medium (see methods below)
7. Place under a humidity dome at 72-78°F, 70-90% RH, under gentle light (T5 fluorescent or low-intensity LED — strong light increases transpiration and causes wilting before roots exist)
8. Mist 1-2x daily; crack the dome slightly after day 3-4 to begin hardening off
9. Roots typically appear in 7-14 days. Once roots are 1-2 inches long, transplant to your growing medium.

**Sanitation is critical.** Unclean tools, contaminated mediums, or dirty water are the most common causes of cloning failure. Use a fresh razor blade or sterilize with isopropyl alcohol between cuts. Use clean water. Use fresh rooting plugs.

### Cloning methods compared

**Rooting medium method (most common):**
- Insert cutting into pre-moistened rockwool cubes, Rapid Rooters, peat pellets, or coco plugs
- Place inside a humidity dome on a tray
- Roots typically appear in 7-14 days
- Best balance of simplicity, cost, and success rate — recommended for beginners
- Popular media: Rapid Rooters (foam plugs, excellent success rate), rockwool cubes (cheap, consistent, need pre-soaking to pH 5.5), Root Riot plugs

**Aeroponic cloning:**
- Cuttings are suspended in a chamber where stems are periodically misted with oxygenated water or light nutrient solution
- Stems get both moisture and abundant oxygen simultaneously, which accelerates root formation
- Roots typically appear in 5-10 days — faster and often more vigorous than medium methods
- Produces uniform, well-developed root systems
- Popular cloners: EZ-Cloner, TurboKlone, Oxyclone, or DIY builds with a pump and spray heads
- **Drawback**: Requires meticulous sanitation — warm, moist chambers breed bacteria and algae fast. Clean the cloner with hydrogen peroxide or bleach solution between runs. Monitor water temperature (68-72°F ideal).
- Best for: growers taking many clones regularly, commercial operations

**Water cloning (simplest method):**
- Place cuttings in a glass or cup of plain water (pH'd to 5.8-6.5, room temperature)
- Change water every 2-3 days to keep it oxygenated and prevent stagnation
- No rooting hormone needed (though it helps)
- Slowest method — roots can take 2-3+ weeks
- Lower success rate than other methods, but it works and costs nothing
- Best for: casual/experimental cloning, "I just want to see if this works"

### Cloning from flowering plants

It's possible to take clones from a plant that's already in flower, but the cutting must revert to vegetative growth before resuming normal development. This process (called "monster cropping") produces clones with unusual, rounded single-blade leaves at first. After 2-4 weeks of veg light (18/6+), normal growth patterns return. Monster-cropped clones often grow very bushy with many branching sites, which can be an advantage.

Use this as a last resort to preserve genetics you forgot to clone during veg — it's slow and has a lower success rate than cloning from a vegging mother.

### Mother plant maintenance

- Keep mothers in veg (18/6 or 20/4) indefinitely
- Prune regularly to encourage bushy growth and provide clone sites
- Feed at moderate strength — overfed mothers produce weaker clones
- Replace mother plants every 6-12 months, or when vigor declines
- Some growers keep mothers in smaller pots (3-5 gallon) to limit size
- Maintain strict pest and disease prevention — any infection in the mother transfers directly to every clone

### Genetic diversity and long-term considerations

Cloning preserves elite genetics, but it also preserves identical vulnerabilities. Every clone shares the same pest/disease susceptibility as the mother. If powdery mildew takes down one plant, it can take them all.

Some growers report reduced vigor after many generations of cloning from clones (rather than from seed-grown mothers), though this is debated. To mitigate:
- Periodically grow new mother plants from seed to refresh genetics
- "Clone from a clone" is fine for several generations, but consider resetting from seed every year or two
- Keep your mother plant healthy — accumulated stress in the mother compounds through subsequent clones
- For long-term genetic preservation, consider tissue culture (advanced technique) or simply keeping reliable seed stock on hand

## Veg phase strategy

The vegetative phase is where you build the plant's structure. Every training decision happens here.

**Duration planning:**
- Minimum veg: 4-6 weeks from seed (or 2-3 weeks from rooted clone)
- SOG: minimal veg, 1-2 weeks from clone
- Standard indoor: 4-8 weeks of veg
- Large plant / ScrOG: 8-12+ weeks of veg
- Mother plants: indefinite veg

**Training in veg (all techniques are safe during veg):**

*Topping:*
- Cut the main stem above the 3rd-5th node. This splits the main cola into two, creating a more even canopy.
- Wait for the plant to recover (5-7 days) before topping again or applying additional stress.
- Can top multiple times — "manifolding" or "mainlining" involves topping down to the 3rd node, growing out two branches, then topping each of those, creating 4, then 8 even tops.

*FIMming:*
- Instead of cutting the full stem tip cleanly, pinch/cut ~80% of the new growth tip.
- Less precise than topping but can produce 3-4 new tops instead of 2.
- Faster recovery than topping, slightly less reliable results.

*LST:*
- Bend and tie branches horizontally to create an even canopy.
- Use soft plant ties, garden wire, or pipe cleaners — avoid anything that can cut into the stem.
- Start when the plant has 4-5 nodes; continue throughout veg.

*Supercropping (HST):*
- Gently crush/bend a branch at a 90° angle — you want to damage the inner fibers without breaking the outer skin.
- The plant repairs the bend with a "knuckle" that becomes a nutrient superhighway.
- Useful for controlling height of individual branches that outgrow the canopy.

*ScrOG:*
- Place a screen/net 8-12 inches above the pot, with 2-4 inch squares.
- As branches grow through the screen, tuck them back under and guide them horizontally.
- Continue tucking until the screen is ~70% full, then flip to 12/12.
- During the stretch, continue tucking for 1-2 weeks into flower.

## Flowering phase

**The stretch (weeks 1-3 of flower):**
- After flipping to 12/12, most strains stretch 50-100% in height over 2-3 weeks
- Sativa-dominant strains can stretch 200%+
- Continue light LST/ScrOG tucking during this period
- Transition nutrients: shift from veg (high N) to bloom (high P-K) over the first 1-2 weeks of flower

**Bud development (weeks 3-6):**
- Calyxes form and stack; pistils emerge from bud sites
- Terpene production begins — smell increases significantly
- Full bloom nutrient strength
- Consider a mid-flower defoliation around day 21 of flower (remove fan leaves shading bud sites — known as "schwazzing" when done aggressively; controversial but many growers see results)

**Ripening (weeks 6-8+ depending on strain):**
- Trichomes develop and mature (clear → cloudy → amber)
- Indica-dominant: typically finish in 7-9 weeks of flower
- Hybrid: 8-10 weeks
- Sativa-dominant: 10-14+ weeks (some haze strains can run 16+ weeks)
- Begin flush or taper nutrients 1-2 weeks before planned harvest
- Lower temperature and humidity for terpene preservation and bud rot prevention

## Revegging

If you want to take clones from a flowering plant or preserve genetics post-harvest, you can reveg:

1. Harvest the top 2/3 of the plant, leaving some lower branches with foliage intact
2. Switch the light schedule back to 18/6 or 20/4
3. Return to veg-strength nutrients (high N)
4. The plant will produce odd, rounded single-blade leaves at first — this is normal
5. After 4-8 weeks, normal growth resumes
6. You can then clone or re-flower the plant

Revegging is slow and somewhat stressful for the plant, but it's a legitimate way to preserve genetics you didn't clone during veg.

## Common photoperiod mistakes

1. **Flipping too late in a small space** — Forgetting the stretch means your plant hits the ceiling. Rule of thumb: flip at 40-50% of your max height.
2. **Light leaks during flower** — Even brief light exposure during the dark period can cause hermaphroditism or revegging. Seal your tent/room completely. Test by standing inside with all lights off — you should see nothing.
3. **Over-vegging without training** — A tall, untopped plant with one main cola wastes light and space. Train during veg to build a wide, even canopy.
4. **Neglecting mother plant health** — Unhealthy mothers produce weak clones. Feed, prune, and monitor your mothers like any other plant.
5. **Not defoliating enough (or too much)** — Some leaf removal improves airflow and light penetration to lower bud sites. But stripping a plant bare stresses it heavily. Find the middle ground.
6. **Impatience at harvest** — Photoperiod strains often take 8-10+ weeks to fully finish flowering. Harvesting early leaves potency and weight on the table. Wait for the trichomes to tell you.
