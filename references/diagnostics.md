# Cannabis Plant Diagnostics

## Diagnostic approach

When a grower reports a problem, resist the urge to jump to a nutrient deficiency diagnosis. Work through this checklist in order — the most common causes are environmental, not nutritional.

### Step 1: Environment

| Check | Target Range | Symptoms if Off |
|---|---|---|
| Temperature | 70-82°F veg, 65-78°F flower | Heat: taco/canoe leaves, foxtailing, bleaching. Cold: slow growth, purple stems, nutrient lockout |
| Humidity | 55-70% veg, 35-50% flower | High: powdery mildew, bud rot. Low: faster transpiration, potential Ca issues |
| VPD | 0.8-1.2 veg, 1.0-1.5 flower | Out of range = stressed stomata, poor nutrient uptake |
| Airflow | Gentle movement, leaves should slightly rustle | Still air = pest/mold haven. Direct fan = wind burn (clawing, thick stems) |
| Light distance/intensity | Strain and fixture dependent | Too close: bleaching, taco leaves, foxtailing. Too far: stretching |

### Step 2: pH

**This is the #1 cause of nutrient problems.** Before diagnosing any deficiency, confirm pH is correct.

| Medium | Input pH | Acceptable Runoff pH |
|---|---|---|
| Soil | 6.0-6.8 (target 6.5) | 6.0-7.0 |
| Coco coir | 5.5-6.5 (target 5.8-6.0) | 5.5-6.5 |
| Hydro (DWC, etc.) | 5.5-6.5 (target 5.8) | N/A — monitor reservoir pH directly |

**pH lockout chart** — nutrients become unavailable outside their optimal pH range:
- Nitrogen: locks out below 5.5 and above 7.5
- Phosphorus: locks out below 5.5 and above 7.0
- Potassium: locks out below 5.5 and above 7.5
- Calcium: locks out below 6.0 (severe below 5.5)
- Magnesium: locks out below 5.5 and above 7.0
- Iron: locks out above 6.5 (this is why iron deficiency is common in high-pH soil)
- Manganese: locks out above 6.5

If pH is off, correct it before adding more nutrients. "Feeding through a lockout" just makes it worse.

### Step 3: Watering

**Overwatering symptoms:**
- Droopy, heavy-looking leaves (not wilting — drooping)
- Slow growth
- Soil stays wet for days
- May develop root rot (brown, slimy roots; sour smell)
- Fungus gnats thrive in constantly wet topsoil

**Underwatering symptoms:**
- Wilting, thin, papery leaves
- Dry, light pot
- Leaves may curl upward
- Recovers quickly after watering

**The lift test:** Pick up the pot. If it's heavy, don't water. If it's light, water. This is more reliable than moisture meters, soil probes, or finger tests. Learn what your pot feels like when fully watered vs. dry.

**Watering technique:**
- Water to 10-20% runoff every time (especially in coco and salt-based nutrients)
- Check runoff pH and EC — if runoff EC is significantly higher than input EC, salt buildup is occurring
- In soil: let the top 1-2 inches dry between waterings
- In coco: water more frequently — coco should never fully dry out. Multiple fertigation events per day is common in mature plants.

### Step 4: Nutrients

Only after ruling out environment, pH, and watering should you consider nutrient issues.

### Essential nutrient classification

Cannabis requires 14 essential mineral nutrients. Understanding the categories helps with diagnosis — macronutrients are needed in large quantities and cause the most visible problems, while micronutrient issues are rarer but just as impactful when they occur.

| Category | Nutrients | Role Summary |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary Macronutrients** | Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), Potassium (K) | The big three — structure, energy, and cellular function. Listed on every fertilizer label as N-P-K ratio. |
| **Secondary Macronutrients** | Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg), Sulfur (S) | Cell wall strength, chlorophyll production, enzyme activation. Often overlooked but frequently deficient. |
| **Micronutrients** | Iron (Fe), Manganese (Mn), Zinc (Zn), Copper (Cu), Boron (B), Molybdenum (Mo), Chlorine (Cl), Nickel (Ni) | Required in trace amounts. Enzyme co-factors, hormone synthesis, nutrient transport. Just as essential as macros — "micro" refers to quantity needed, not importance. |
| **Beneficial (non-essential)** | Silicon (Si), Cobalt (Co), Sodium (Na), Selenium (Se) | Not strictly essential but improve stress tolerance, structural strength, and pest resistance. Silicon is the most relevant for cannabis. |

Plants also need Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), and Oxygen (O) from air and water — these aren't supplemented.

**Key concept — nutrient mobility determines where symptoms appear:**
- **Mobile nutrients** (N, P, K, Mg): The plant can move these from old leaves to new growth when supply runs short. Deficiency symptoms appear on **lower/older** leaves first.
- **Immobile nutrients** (Ca, Fe, Mn, Zn, Cu, B, Mo, S): The plant cannot redistribute these. Deficiency symptoms appear on **upper/new** growth first.

### What each nutrient does — functional roles

This table shows which plant processes each nutrient participates in. When diagnosing issues, this helps connect a symptom (e.g., slow root growth) to the nutrients involved (P, Ca, B, Zn).

| Plant Function | Primary Macros (N-P-K) | Secondary (Ca, Mg, S) | Micronutrients | Beneficial |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Nitrogen metabolism** | N, P | — | Cu, Ni, Mo | Co, Si |
| **Photosynthesis** | N, P, K | Mg, S | Cu, Fe, Zn, Mn, Mo | Si |
| **Disease resistance** | K | Ca, S | Cu, Cl, Zn, Ni, Mn | Si, Se, Na |
| **Stress tolerance** (heat, drought, cold) | K | Ca | B, Cu, Cl, Zn, Ni, Mn | Si, Se, Na |
| **Carbohydrate production** | N, P, K | Mg, S | Mn | — |
| **Protein production** | N | S | B, Mn | — |
| **Oil/resin production** | P | S | B | — |
| **Vegetative growth** | N, P, K | — | — | — |
| **Hormone metabolism** | — | — | Zn, Mo | — |
| **Root growth** | P | Ca | B, Zn | — |
| **Energy transfer** (ATP) | P, K | S | B, Fe | — |
| **Nutrient uptake** | P, K | S | B, Cl | — |
| **Water regulation** | P, K | — | B, Cl | — |

**Key takeaways for cannabis growers:**
- **Phosphorus** is involved in nearly every function — energy, roots, flowering, nutrient uptake. This is why P deficiency in flower is devastating.
- **Potassium** is critical for stress tolerance, disease resistance, water regulation, and carbohydrate transport. Heavy-flowering plants consume large amounts of K.
- **Sulfur** participates in photosynthesis, protein/oil production, energy transfer, and disease resistance — it's more important than many growers realize, especially for terpene production (many terpenes contain sulfur compounds).
- **Boron** supports root growth, stress tolerance, energy transfer, water regulation, and oil production — a lot of critical functions for a micronutrient. This is why boron deficiency (though rare) can be so damaging.
- **Silicon** shows up in photosynthesis, nitrogen use, disease resistance, and stress tolerance — it's the most broadly beneficial of the non-essential elements.
- **Zinc** is involved in hormone metabolism (auxin synthesis), which is why zinc deficiency causes stunted, distorted growth — the plant can't regulate its own growth hormones properly.

## Nutrient Deficiency Guide

### Mobile nutrients (symptoms appear on LOWER/OLDER leaves first)
The plant moves these nutrients from old leaves to new growth when supply is short.

**Nitrogen (N) Deficiency:**
- Lower leaves turn uniformly pale green, then yellow, then die and fall off
- Progresses upward if uncorrected
- Overall slow growth, small leaves
- Common in: late veg, early flower (especially if bloom feed is too low in N)
- Fix: increase N in feed. Calmag products typically don't contain N — use a veg nutrient or standalone N source.

**Phosphorus (P) Deficiency:**
- Dark green or blue-green leaves
- Purple/red stems and petioles (note: some strains are naturally purple-stemmed)
- Brown/rust spots or patches on lower leaves
- Slow growth, delayed flowering
- Common in: cold temperatures (below 60°F), high pH (above 7.0)
- Fix: check pH first (P locks out in high pH soil). Increase P in feed. Bone meal for organic growers.

**Potassium (K) Deficiency:**
- Brown, crispy leaf edges (margins) — looks like the leaf is burning from the outside in
- Starts on lower leaves
- Leaves may curl or cup
- Can resemble light burn — rule out light distance first
- Common in: heavy flower, high EC environments
- Fix: increase K. Many bloom boosters are high in K.

**Magnesium (Mg) Deficiency:**
- Interveinal chlorosis on lower/middle leaves — veins stay green, tissue between veins turns yellow
- Rust/brown spots may develop
- Very common in coco coir grows and when using RO/distilled water
- Fix: Cal-Mag supplement (2-5 mL/gal depending on brand). Epsom salt foliar spray (1 tsp/gal) for quick relief.

### Immobile nutrients (symptoms appear on UPPER/NEW growth first)
The plant can't move these nutrients, so new growth suffers when supply runs short.

**Calcium (Ca) Deficiency:**
- Brown spots on new growth, often irregular shapes
- New leaves may be small, crinkled, or deformed
- Stems may be weak or hollow
- Very common in: coco coir, RO water, low-calcium tap water
- Fix: Cal-Mag supplement. Check pH — calcium locks out below 6.0.

**Iron (Fe) Deficiency:**
- Bright yellow new growth with green veins (interveinal chlorosis on upper leaves)
- Distinct from Mg deficiency because it affects NEW growth, not old
- Almost always a pH problem — iron locks out above 6.5
- Fix: lower pH. Iron supplements are rarely needed if pH is correct. EDDHA iron chelate if supplementing.

**Sulfur (S) Deficiency:**
- New leaves turn uniformly lime green to yellow (not interveinal — the whole leaf)
- Resembles nitrogen deficiency but on upper leaves instead of lower
- Uncommon with most commercial nutrients
- Fix: Epsom salt (contains sulfur and magnesium). Most tap water contains enough sulfur.

**Manganese (Mn) Deficiency:**
- Interveinal chlorosis on new growth with tan/brown spots
- Can look similar to calcium deficiency
- Usually caused by high pH (above 6.5)
- Fix: correct pH. Rarely need to supplement directly.

**Zinc (Zn) Deficiency:**
- New leaves are small, thin, and crinkled with interveinal chlorosis
- Leaf tips may die back; growth is stunted
- Internodal spacing decreases (leaves cluster together)
- Immobile — symptoms appear on new growth
- More common than expected in cannabis, especially in high-pH environments or when phosphorus is excessive (P antagonizes Zn uptake)
- Fix: correct pH to 6.0-6.5. Zinc sulfate foliar spray for quick correction. Reduce phosphorus if over-feeding bloom boosters.

**Copper (Cu) Deficiency:**
- New leaves wilt and may develop dark blue or purple undertones
- Leaf tips and edges can turn yellow or develop a copper-grey color
- Buds may ripen very slowly or not mature properly
- Rare in most grows — most nutrients and media contain sufficient copper
- Usually caused by pH being too high (above 7.0) rather than actual absence
- Fix: correct pH. Copper-containing fungicides (copper sulfate) can supplement if truly deficient, but be cautious — copper toxicity is more common than deficiency.

**Boron (B) Deficiency:**
- Growing tips appear abnormally thick, twisted, or die off
- New leaves show brown/yellow spotting and may be brittle
- Stems can become hollow or cracked
- Root growth is stunted
- Boron is involved in cell wall formation, root elongation, and seed/flower development
- Rare with commercial nutrients, but can occur in high-pH conditions (above 6.8) or when calcium is excessive
- Fix: correct pH. One teaspoon of boric acid per gallon of water as a one-time drench if supplementing — be very careful with dosing, as boron toxicity happens easily (burnt leaf margins, brown spots).

**Molybdenum (Mo) Deficiency:**
- Middle to older leaves develop interveinal chlorosis that can progress to marginal scorching
- Leaves may curl or twist
- Resembles nitrogen deficiency because molybdenum is required for the enzyme that converts nitrate to ammonium — without Mo, nitrogen uptake is impaired
- Very rare — plants need only trace amounts
- Most commonly caused by low pH (below 5.5) — Mo is one of the few nutrients that becomes MORE available at higher pH
- Fix: raise pH if too low. Most quality nutrient lines contain sufficient Mo.

### Beneficial element

**Silicon (Si):**
- Not essential but highly beneficial — strengthens cell walls, improves heat and drought tolerance, enhances pest resistance (tougher leaves are harder for pests to penetrate)
- Deficiency symptoms: weaker stems and branches, plants may struggle to support their own weight in flower, increased susceptibility to pests and environmental stress
- More commonly deficient in indoor grows (outdoor plants access silicon from soil minerals naturally)
- Common supplements: potassium silicate (Silica Blast, Power Si, ArmorSi). Add to water FIRST before other nutrients, as it raises pH significantly.
- Silica products must be added to water before pH adjustment and before other nutrients — it precipitates if mixed with concentrated nutrients directly.

## Nutrient interaction and antagonism

Understanding that nutrients interact with each other is critical for avoiding misdiagnosis:

- **Excess potassium** locks out calcium, magnesium, manganese, zinc, and iron. Heavy bloom boosters are a common cause.
- **Excess phosphorus** locks out zinc, iron, and copper. Over-using PK boosters in flower creates false micronutrient deficiencies.
- **Excess calcium** can lock out magnesium, boron, and potassium.
- **Excess nitrogen** can suppress potassium and calcium uptake.
- **Iron, manganese, zinc, and copper** compete with each other for uptake — excess of one can cause deficiency in another.

The lesson: more nutrients is not always better. Balanced feeding at moderate EC, with correct pH, prevents most problems. When you see a deficiency, the cause may not be "not enough of X" but "too much of Y blocking X."

## Nutrient Toxicity

**Nitrogen toxicity** (most common toxicity):
- Very dark green, almost blackish-green leaves
- "Clawing" — leaf tips curl downward like a claw
- Slow bud development in flower
- Leaves may feel leathery or waxy
- Fix: flush with pH'd water, reduce N in feed. In flower, ensure you've transitioned off veg nutrients.

**General nutrient burn:**
- Brown, crispy leaf tips — the "tip burn" pattern
- Progresses inward from tips if uncorrected
- Caused by EC/PPM being too high
- Fix: reduce feed by 25-50%. If severe, flush with pH'd water at 1/2 strength, then resume at a lower dose.

## Pest Identification

### Spider Mites
- Tiny dots (stippling) on leaf tops — turn leaf over to see the mites
- Fine webbing between leaves and branches (heavy infestation)
- Leaves look "dusty" or speckled
- Thrive in hot, dry conditions (above 80°F, below 40% RH)
- **Treatment:** Neem oil (veg only), insecticidal soap, spinosad, predatory mites (Phytoseiulus persimilis). In flower: Dr. Zymes, Lost Coast Plant Therapy, or Trifecta Crop Control (avoid neem on buds). Treat every 3-5 days for 2-3 weeks — you must break the egg cycle.

### Fungus Gnats
- Small dark flies hovering near soil surface
- Larvae feed on roots in the top layer of soil
- Indicate overwatering — they breed in consistently wet topsoil
- **Treatment:** Let soil dry out between waterings. Sticky traps for adults. Mosquito Bits (BTI) or Gnatrol for larvae. A layer of sand or perlite on the soil surface deters egg-laying.

### Thrips
- Silver/bronze streaks or patches on leaves
- Tiny elongated insects on leaf surfaces
- Black dots (frass) on leaves
- **Treatment:** Spinosad (Captain Jack's Deadbug), insecticidal soap, predatory mites (Amblyseius cucumeris). Blue sticky traps.

### Aphids
- Clusters of small soft-bodied insects, usually on new growth and undersides of leaves
- Can be green, black, white, or pink
- Produce honeydew (sticky residue) that can lead to sooty mold
- **Treatment:** Neem oil, insecticidal soap, ladybugs, lacewings. Strong spray of water knocks them off temporarily.

### Broad Mites / Russet Mites
- Invisible to the naked eye — need 60x+ magnification
- New growth looks twisted, wet, or glossy
- Leaves cup upward or become stiff and brittle
- Often misdiagnosed as pH issues or genetics
- **Treatment:** Difficult. Avid (abamectin) is effective but harsh. Sulfur burns, Nuke Em, or predatory mites (Amblyseius andersoni). Multiple applications required.

## Disease Identification

### Powdery Mildew (PM)
- White powdery patches on leaf surfaces
- Thrives in high humidity, poor airflow, and temperature swings
- Can spread to buds — contaminated flower should not be consumed
- **Prevention:** Keep humidity below 55% in flower, ensure good airflow, defoliate for air circulation
- **Treatment:** Potassium bicarbonate spray, milk spray (40% milk / 60% water), Lost Coast Plant Therapy, Regalia. Remove heavily affected leaves. Neem oil in veg.

### Bud Rot (Botrytis cinerea)
- Gray or brown mold inside dense buds
- Affected buds feel mushy; leaves within the bud turn yellow and pull out easily
- Often starts inside the densest colas where moisture gets trapped
- **Prevention:** Humidity below 45% in late flower, good airflow around and through the canopy, defoliation for air circulation, avoid overhead watering in flower
- **Treatment:** Remove affected buds immediately (cut below the affected area). Inspect surrounding buds daily. Cannot be treated once established — prevention only.

### Root Rot (Pythium)
- Brown, slimy roots (healthy roots are white)
- Foul, sour smell from root zone
- Drooping despite wet medium
- Slow growth, yellowing
- Common in DWC, overwatered soil, and non-aerated hydro
- **Treatment:** Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) root drench (3% solution, 1 mL per liter). Hydroguard (beneficial bacteria) for prevention. In DWC: keep water temperature below 68°F, ensure adequate aeration.

## Hermaphroditism

Cannabis plants can develop both male and female reproductive parts under stress. This is called "herming" or going hermaphrodite.

**Causes:**
- Light leaks during flower (photoperiods)
- Heat stress (above 85°F consistently)
- Light stress (too intense, inconsistent schedule)
- Physical damage during flower
- Genetics (some strains are more prone)
- Root zone issues (severely rootbound, root rot)

**Signs:**
- "Nanners" — small yellow banana-shaped structures protruding from buds (stamens). These release pollen immediately.
- Pollen sacs — round balls on stalks at the nodes, similar to male pre-flowers.

**What to do:**
- A few nanners late in flower: pluck them out carefully (don't shake pollen loose) and monitor daily. Harvest soon.
- Widespread pollen sacs: the plant is male-dominant hermie. Remove it from the grow space to protect other plants.
- Seeded buds are smokeable but less potent and less pleasant. The seeds from a hermed plant often carry the hermie trait — don't use them for future grows.
