# Equipment Setup & Water Quality Guide

## Tent sizing and plant count

Choosing the right tent size is one of the first decisions. Here's a practical mapping:

| Tent Size | Area (sq ft) | Typical Plant Count | Recommended Light Wattage (LED) | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2' x 2' | 4 | 1-2 plants | 100-150W | First grow, single plant learning |
| 2' x 4' | 8 | 2-4 plants | 200-300W | Great starter size, fits in a closet |
| 3' x 3' | 9 | 2-4 plants | 250-350W | Good balance of space and efficiency |
| 4' x 4' | 16 | 4-6 plants | 400-650W | Most popular size for home growers |
| 5' x 5' | 25 | 6-9 plants | 600-1000W | Serious hobby or small personal supply |
| 4' x 8' | 32 | 8-12 plants | 800-1200W (2 lights) | Dedicated grow room replacement |

**Height matters too.** Most tents come in 60" (5') or 80" (6'8") heights. Always go taller if you can — you lose height to the light, the hanging hardware, the pot, and the gap between light and canopy. A 5-foot tent can feel tight with tall sativa-dominant strains. 80" is strongly recommended for most setups.

**Plant count is flexible.** Fewer larger plants (longer veg, more training) or more smaller plants (SOG) can fill the same space. The limiting factor is light footprint, not arbitrary plant count.

## Lighting deep dive

### Light types compared

| Light Type | Pros | Cons | Cost (approx) | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| LED (Quantum Board / Bar Style) | Most efficient (µmol/J), low heat, long lifespan, full spectrum, dimmable | Higher upfront cost | $100-600+ | Everyone — the current standard |
| HPS (High Pressure Sodium) | Proven performer, deep canopy penetration, cheaper upfront | Hot, high energy use, needs ballast, single spectrum | $80-200 | Budget builds, cold climates (heat is a feature) |
| CMH/LEC (Ceramic Metal Halide) | Full spectrum, good efficiency, great for veg and flower | Moderate heat, bulb replacement | $150-400 | Growers wanting HID quality with better spectrum |
| CFL/T5 Fluorescent | Low heat, cheap, good for seedlings/clones | Weak for flower, low penetration | $20-80 | Seedlings, clones, supplemental side lighting only |

### LED sizing rule of thumb

For flowering cannabis with modern LED fixtures:
- **Minimum**: 30W per square foot (will grow cannabis, but yields will be modest)
- **Good**: 35-40W per square foot (solid results for most growers)
- **Optimal**: 40-50W per square foot (maximizes yield in the space)
- **With CO2**: 50-65W per square foot (only worthwhile with sealed room and CO2 supplementation)

These are **actual wall draw** watts, not "equivalent" or "rated" watts that some budget lights advertise. Always check the actual power consumption spec.

### Recommended budget and mid-tier LED lights

Budget-friendly options that the community consistently recommends (these change over time, but the brands have track records):
- **Budget**: Mars Hydro TS series, Spider Farmer SF series, Viparspectra XS series
- **Mid-tier**: HLG (Horticulture Lighting Group), Kingbrite (Alibaba, Samsung LM301 diodes), AC Infinity Ionboard
- **High-end**: Gavita, Fluence, Luxx, Growers Choice ROI-E

When evaluating lights, look for:
- **Efficacy** (µmol/J): 2.0+ is acceptable, 2.5+ is good, 2.8+ is excellent
- **Samsung LM301B/H diodes** or equivalent — the industry standard for efficiency
- **Meanwell drivers** — reliable, efficient, long-lasting
- **Dimmable** — essential for adjusting intensity through growth stages
- **Even light spread** — bar-style fixtures generally outperform single-panel for uniformity

### Light spectrum: blue vs red

Cannabis responds to different wavelengths of light at different growth stages. While modern "full spectrum" white LEDs work great for the entire lifecycle, understanding spectrum helps you optimize.

**Blue light (400-500nm) — vegetative growth:**
- Promotes compact, bushy growth with shorter internodes
- Encourages leaf and stem development
- Helps prevent stretching
- T5 fluorescents and "veg" switches on older LEDs emphasize blue
- Metal halide (MH) bulbs are blue-dominant — traditionally used for veg with HID setups

**Red light (600-700nm) — flowering:**
- Drives photosynthesis most efficiently (peaks at ~660nm)
- Triggers and sustains flowering (phytochrome response)
- Promotes bud development and stretch
- HPS bulbs are red-dominant — traditionally used for flower
- "Bloom" switches on older LEDs emphasize red

**Far-red (700-780nm):**
- Influences the phytochrome balance that controls flowering initiation
- Can increase stretch if overrepresented
- Some growers use a brief far-red burst at lights-off to accelerate the transition to "dark mode" (the Emerson effect)

**UV (280-400nm):**
- May increase trichome production (the plant produces more resin as a UV defense mechanism)
- Some growers add supplemental UV-B bars during flower for this reason
- Debated whether the trichome gains justify the cost and bulb replacement

**Practical takeaway:** Most modern full-spectrum white LEDs (3000K-3500K with added red diodes) work well for both veg and flower. You don't need to swap lights between stages anymore. If your light has a dimmer, run it lower in veg and higher in flower — intensity matters more than spectrum for most home growers.

### Grow light distance chart

These are starting guidelines — every fixture is different. Always check the manufacturer's recommendations and adjust based on plant response (stretching = too far, bleaching/taco leaves = too close).

| Growth Stage | LED (200-400W) | LED (400W+) | HPS 600W | HPS 1000W |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Seedling | 28-36" | 36-48" | 36-48" | 48-60" |
| Early veg | 24-30" | 30-36" | 30-36" | 36-48" |
| Late veg | 18-24" | 24-30" | 24-30" | 30-36" |
| Flower | 12-18" | 18-24" | 18-24" | 24-30" |

**Adjust by plant response, not just distance:**
- **Stretching** (long internodes, reaching toward the light): Light is too far or too dim. Lower it 2-3 inches.
- **Bleaching** (white/pale tops closest to light): Too close. Raise 3-4 inches.
- **Taco leaves** (edges curling upward like a taco shell): Light/heat stress. Raise the light and check temperature at canopy level.
- **Foxtailing** (new calyxes stacking on top of buds irregularly): Can be genetic, but if only happening on the colas closest to the light, it's light stress.

**Use a PPFD meter or phone app** (Photone is popular) to measure actual light intensity at the canopy. Target numbers by stage:
- Seedling: 200-300 PPFD
- Veg: 400-600 PPFD
- Flower: 600-900 PPFD (up to 1000-1400 with CO2 supplementation)

## Ventilation and odor control

### The ventilation formula

The basic principle: your inline fan should be able to exchange the air in your tent at least once per minute, and ideally every 1-3 minutes.

**Calculate tent volume**: Length x Width x Height (in feet) = cubic feet

**Example**: A 4x4x6.5' tent = 104 cubic feet. An inline fan rated at 200+ CFM could exchange that air roughly twice per minute.

**Carbon filter drag**: A carbon filter reduces your fan's effective CFM by roughly 25-30%. Factor this in. A 400 CFM fan with a carbon filter effectively delivers ~280-300 CFM.

### Recommended fan sizes by tent

| Tent Size | Minimum Fan Size | Recommended Fan Size |
|---|---|---|
| 2x2 | 4" | 4" (with speed controller) |
| 2x4 or 3x3 | 4" | 4-6" |
| 4x4 | 6" | 6" |
| 5x5 | 6" | 6-8" |
| 4x8 | 8" | 8" (or dual 6") |

### Complete ventilation setup

A proper ventilation system consists of:
1. **Inline exhaust fan** — pulls air out of the tent (AC Infinity Cloudline series is the community favorite for its built-in controller, temperature/humidity triggers, and quiet operation)
2. **Carbon filter** — connects to the inline fan inside the tent to scrub odor before exhausting. Replace every 1-2 grows (12-18 months). Phresh, AC Infinity, and Vivosun are popular brands.
3. **Ducting** — connects the fan/filter to the tent's exhaust port. Use insulated ducting to reduce noise. Keep runs short and straight — every bend reduces CFM.
4. **Passive intake** — most growers leave the lower tent vents open for passive intake rather than running a second intake fan. This creates negative pressure (the tent walls should suck inward slightly), which ensures all air exits through the carbon filter.
5. **Oscillating clip fans** — 1-2 inside the tent for air circulation at canopy level. Leaves should gently rustle, not whip. Strengthens stems and prevents microclimates/mold.

### Odor management

Cannabis smells — a lot — especially from mid-flower onward. A properly sized carbon filter handles 95%+ of the odor.

Common odor mistakes:
- **Undersized carbon filter**: Match the filter to the fan size (6" fan = 6" filter)
- **Exhausting inside the house**: Vent outside if possible (window, dryer vent, attic)
- **Old carbon filter**: Activated carbon loses effectiveness after 12-18 months. If you smell it, the carbon is spent.
- **Positive pressure in the tent**: The tent walls should pull slightly inward. If the tent is ballooning outward, unfiltered air is escaping through gaps.

## Water quality

Water quality is a topic beginners overlook but it has a huge impact on plant health and nutrient availability.

### Know your water source

**Tap water:**
- Test your baseline PPM/EC before adding nutrients. Most municipal water ranges from 50-400 PPM.
- Below 200 PPM: generally fine for cannabis. You may not need Cal-Mag if your water has decent calcium/magnesium naturally.
- 200-400 PPM: usable but you're starting with a nutrient load. Reduce your added nutrients accordingly.
- Above 400 PPM: consider a filter. High PPM tap water can contain excess chlorine, chloramine, sodium, or heavy metals.
- **Chlorine**: Dissipates if you let water sit uncovered for 24 hours, or instantly with a few drops of dechlorinator.
- **Chloramine**: Does NOT off-gas like chlorine. Requires a carbon filter, Campden tablets, or ascorbic acid to remove. Check your local water report to see which your municipality uses.

**Reverse Osmosis (RO) water:**
- Starts at 0-10 PPM — a blank slate
- Gives you total control over what the plant receives
- **MUST add Cal-Mag** — RO water has zero calcium and magnesium, which the plant needs
- Typical Cal-Mag addition: 2-5 mL/gal to bring calcium to ~150 PPM and magnesium to ~50 PPM before adding other nutrients
- More work but eliminates variable water quality as a problem source
- Recommended for coco coir grows especially, since coco naturally binds calcium

**Well water:**
- Highly variable — test it. Can be excellent or terrible.
- May contain iron, sulfur, or high mineral content
- Get a water quality report or use a TDS meter as a starting point

### Water temperature

- **Target**: 65-72°F (18-22°C) for soil/coco watering
- **DWC/hydro reservoir**: Keep below 68°F (20°C) to prevent root rot. Water chillers are common in DWC setups.
- Cold water can shock roots; very warm water holds less dissolved oxygen

### pH tools

Every grower needs a way to measure and adjust pH. This is non-negotiable.

- **pH meter**: Apera PH20 or Bluelab pH Pen are reliable mid-range options. Cheap yellow Amazon meters work but drift quickly and need frequent calibration.
- **pH up/down**: General Hydroponics pH Up and pH Down are the standard. A little goes a long way — add drops, not pours.
- **Calibration solution**: pH 4.0 and 7.0 buffers. Calibrate your meter monthly or whenever readings seem off.
- **pH test drops** (backup): General Hydroponics pH test kit with color-matching drops. Less precise than a meter but never needs calibration and works as a sanity check.

### EC/PPM meters

- **TDS meter** (measures PPM): Essential for monitoring nutrient strength. Cheap ones are fine — they're more reliable than cheap pH meters.
- **Two PPM scales exist**: The 500 scale (multiply EC x 500) and the 700 scale (multiply EC x 700). Know which your meter uses. Most US-market meters use the 500 scale. When sharing PPM numbers with others, specify your scale or just use EC.
- **EC (Electrical Conductivity)** is the universal standard — measured in mS/cm or µS/cm. If you can, learn to think in EC rather than PPM to avoid conversion confusion.

## Grow medium comparison

### Soil

**Pros**: Most forgiving, buffers pH, can provide nutrients for weeks (if using amended/hot soil), doesn't need pH-perfect water as aggressively, natural and familiar.

**Cons**: Slower growth than coco/hydro, harder to correct issues (nutrients are buffered in the soil), can harbor pests (fungus gnats love wet soil), harder to overfeed but also harder to flush.

**Popular soil choices:**
- **Fox Farm Ocean Forest (FFOF)**: Pre-amended, "hot" (nutrient-rich) — can burn seedlings if planted directly. Many growers start seedlings in a milder mix and transplant into FFOF.
- **Fox Farm Happy Frog**: Milder than Ocean Forest, better for seedlings and autoflowers.
- **Roots Organics Original**: Well-draining, lightly amended.
- **Build-a-soil (living soil)**: Pre-mixed living soil with mycorrhizae and amendments. Water-only for most of the grow.
- **DIY supersoil / living soil**: Mix your own with base soil, perlite, worm castings, bone meal, blood meal, kelp meal, etc. "Cook" it for 4-6 weeks before use.

**pH target**: 6.0-6.8 (target 6.5)

### Coco Coir

**Pros**: Faster growth than soil (approaches hydro speeds), excellent drainage and aeration, reusable, inert (full control over nutrients), very hard to overwater.

**Cons**: Requires nutrients from day one (no built-in food), needs frequent watering (multiple times per day for mature plants), Cal-Mag hungry (coco binds calcium), pH must be monitored closely, requires buffered coco (pre-rinsed, calcium-treated).

**Popular coco choices:**
- **Canna Coco Professional Plus**: Pre-buffered, consistent quality. The gold standard.
- **Mother Earth Coco + Perlite**: Good budget option, pre-mixed with perlite.
- **Coco Loco (Fox Farm)**: Coco-based but with some amendments — a hybrid approach.

**Important**: Always use pre-buffered, pre-rinsed coco. Unbuffered coco from garden centers (like the compressed bricks) needs to be rinsed and buffered with Cal-Mag solution before use, or it will steal calcium from your nutrient solution.

**Mix**: 70% coco / 30% perlite is the standard ratio. Some growers go 50/50 for even more drainage and aeration.

**pH target**: 5.5-6.5 (target 5.8-6.0)

**Watering in coco**: Unlike soil, coco should never fully dry out. Water to 10-20% runoff at every feeding. Mature plants in coco often need 2-5 fertigation events per day (this is where automated drip systems shine).

### Hydro (DWC — Deep Water Culture)

**Pros**: Fastest growth rates, maximum control, huge yield potential, clean (no soil pests).

**Cons**: Least forgiving — problems escalate fast. Requires monitoring (pH, EC, water temp, dissolved oxygen) daily or more often. Equipment failure (air pump dies, water gets warm) can kill plants in hours. Steeper learning curve.

**Basic DWC setup**: 5-gallon bucket with net pot lid, air pump, air stone, pH/EC meters, hydroton (clay pebbles) in the net pot. Roots grow down into the nutrient solution.

**pH target**: 5.5-6.5 (target 5.8)

**Not recommended for first grows.** Learn in soil or coco first — understand pH, nutrient ratios, and plant signals before adding the complexity of hydro.

## Seed sourcing

### Seed types

| Seed Type | What It Means | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Regular | Can be male or female (~50/50) | Breeders, experienced growers |
| Feminized | 99%+ female (no males to cull) | Most home growers — eliminates guesswork |
| Autoflower (feminized) | Female + flowers automatically by age | Beginners, fast harvests, limited height |
| F1 Hybrids | First-generation cross — high vigor, consistency | Growers wanting uniformity and performance |

**For beginners**: Buy feminized seeds (photo or auto). Regular seeds mean roughly half your plants will be male and need to be identified and removed before they pollinate your females.

### Reputable seed banks

The r/microgrowery community consistently recommends:
- **North Atlantic Seed Co (NASC)** — US-based, fast shipping, huge selection, excellent customer service
- **Multiversebeans** — US-based, great freebies, community favorite
- **DC Seed Exchange** — US-based, strong selection
- **Seedsman** — EU-based, massive catalog, slower shipping to US
- **Mephisto Genetics** — Autoflower specialists, premium genetics (drops sell out fast)
- **Night Owl Seeds** — Autoflower specialist, created by Daz (ex-Mephisto)
- **Ethos Genetics, In House Genetics, Barney's Farm, Dutch Passion** — established photoperiod breeders

**Bagseed** (seeds found in purchased cannabis): Viable and free, but you won't know the strain, sex (likely regular, not feminized), or whether it carries hermaphrodite genetics. Fine for a learning grow; not ideal if you want reliable results.

## CO2 supplementation

### Do you need it?

**Almost certainly not.** CO2 supplementation is an advanced technique that only provides benefits when ALL other factors are already optimized. It's one of the last things to add, not one of the first.

CO2 supplementation only helps when:
- Light intensity is high (900+ PPFD) — plants can only use extra CO2 if they have enough light to drive the additional photosynthesis
- Temperature is elevated (80-85°F, which would stress plants at ambient CO2 but is optimal at elevated CO2)
- Nutrients, pH, and watering are dialed in
- The room is sealed (no exhaust — CO2 escapes through ventilation)

**At ambient CO2 (~420 PPM)**, cannabis photosynthesis plateaus around 800 PPFD. Extra light above that is wasted without extra CO2.

**At 1200-1500 PPM CO2**, plants can use up to 1400+ PPFD productively, and optimal temperatures shift upward.

### If you do supplement

- **Target**: 1200-1500 PPM during lights-on only. Plants don't photosynthesize in the dark; running CO2 at night is wasted.
- **Methods**: CO2 tank + regulator + timer (most common), CO2 generator (burns propane/natural gas — adds heat and humidity), fermentation bags (minimal, mostly marketing)
- **Measurement**: CO2 monitor/controller is essential — running CO2 above 2000 PPM is toxic to plants and dangerous to humans.
- **Sealed room required**: If you're exhausting air through a carbon filter, you're exhausting your CO2 too. CO2 supplementation requires a sealed room with AC and dehumidifier instead of exhaust ventilation.

**Bottom line for beginners**: Spend your money on better lights, better genetics, and a pH meter before thinking about CO2. It's a 10-15% yield boost in a perfectly optimized environment and a waste of money in a suboptimal one.

## Beginner shopping list

Here's a practical starter setup for a 2x4 or 4x4 tent:

**Essentials (can't grow without these):**
- Grow tent (2x4 or 4x4)
- LED grow light (sized for tent — see wattage chart)
- Inline exhaust fan (AC Infinity Cloudline or equivalent)
- Carbon filter (matched to fan size)
- Ducting (4" or 6", 8-10 feet)
- Timer (mechanical is fine; digital is nicer)
- Fabric pots (3 or 5 gallon)
- Growing medium (soil or coco + perlite)
- Nutrients (starter kit — Fox Farm trio, General Hydroponics Flora trio, or similar)
- pH meter + pH up/down solutions
- TDS/EC meter
- Seeds (feminized, from a reputable seed bank)

**Strongly recommended:**
- Oscillating clip fan(s) for air circulation
- Thermometer/hygrometer (combo units with min/max memory — Govee Bluetooth sensors are great for remote monitoring)
- Jeweler's loupe or USB microscope (60x-120x for trichome checking)
- Pruning shears / trimming scissors (Chikamasa B-500 are the industry standard for trimming)
- Plant ties / soft wire for LST
- Measuring cups/syringes for nutrients
- Cal-Mag supplement (essential for coco and RO water, helpful in soil)
- Saucers or drip trays under pots

**Nice to have:**
- VPD chart or controller
- Trellis netting for ScrOG
- Ratchet hangers for the light (adjustable, much easier than fixed chains)
- Surge protector / power strip with timer
- Drying rack or line

### Approximate budget ranges (2024-2026 USD)

| Budget Level | Total Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Bare minimum | $200-350 | Budget tent, budget LED, basic fan, soil, bag nutrients |
| Good starter | $400-600 | Quality tent, decent LED (Mars/Spider Farmer), AC Infinity fan, good nutrients |
| Solid mid-range | $600-1000 | Quality everything — HLG or equivalent LED, AC Infinity with controller, full nutrient line, meters |
| Premium | $1000-2000+ | Top-tier LED, environmental controllers, autopots or drip system, premium genetics |

The first grow is always the most expensive. Much of the equipment (tent, light, fan, meters) is reusable for years. Recurring costs are mainly seeds, nutrients, growing medium, and replacement carbon filters.
